How To Put in Contacts: A Beginner's Handbook

Understanding Contact Lenses

With the variety of options available, choosing contact lenses can be both exciting and overwhelming. Disposable lenses are particularly popular because they are easy to use and help maintain good hygiene. This type of contact lens is designed for short-term use — often replaced daily or bi-weekly.

Rigid gas permeable (RGP) lenses, though less common, offer unique advantages. Made from durable materials, they provide sharper vision, especially for those with specific needs like astigmatism. These lenses also allow more oxygen to reach the eyes, which can enhance comfort throughout the day.

Selecting the right contact lens is crucial for comfort and clear vision. Your lifestyle, budget, and specific vision requirements should guide your decision. Consulting with an eye care professional can help you make the best choice based on your needs and preferences. Understanding these differences enables you to make informed decisions for a more enjoyable contact lens experience.

Getting Ready to Insert Contact Lenses

Prepare yourself and your environment before inserting contact lenses. Begin by thoroughly washing your hands with soap and water to avoid transferring dirt and germs to your eyes. Dry your hands with a lint-free towel to prevent fibers from sticking to the lenses.

Choose a well-lit area with a mirror, such as a bathroom, where you can stand or sit comfortably. Good lighting helps you see clearly and makes inserting the lenses easier. Keep all necessary supplies at hand, including your lens case, solution, and any other products you use.

If you're new to wearing contacts, take a moment to relax. Deep breaths can help steady your nerves and hands. Inspect your lenses to ensure they are clean and undamaged. Proper preparation enhances comfort and ease of use. By following these steps, you simplify and improve the process.

How to Insert Contact Lenses

Inserting contact lenses might initially seem challenging, but it becomes easier with practice. Follow these simple steps to incorporate it into your daily routine.

1. Wash Your Hands – Clean your hands thoroughly with soap and water, then dry them. This helps prevent dirt or bacteria from entering your eyes.

2. Prepare the Lens – Remove one lens from its case and place it on the tip of your index finger. Ensure it is right side up—it should resemble a bowl with smooth edges. If the edges flare outward, flip it right side out.

3. Position Your Eyelids – Use a finger from your free hand to lift and hold your upper eyelid. The middle finger of the hand with the contact lens to pull down your lower eyelid.

4. Insert the Lens – Look straight ahead or slightly upward, then carefully place the lens on your eye using your index finger. Release your eyelids slowly and blink several times to help the lens settle.

5. Repeat for the Other Eye – Follow the same steps for the other eye.

If the lens feels uncomfortable, remove it and check for any tears or debris. Rinse with solution and try again. With practice, wearing contact lenses will feel natural, allowing you to enjoy sharp vision without glasses. For more detailed guidance, visit our contact lens FAQ page.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

While using contacts, you may encounter some common issues. These issues, along with simple solutions, include:

  • Discomfort – If you experience discomfort or irritation, there may be debris on the lens. To resolve this, remove the lens, rinse it thoroughly with solution, and check for damage before reinserting.
  • Dryness – If a lens will not stay against your eye, it could be due to dryness. Use rewetting drops to moisten the eye, making lens placement easier.
  • Difficulty staying on the eye – If a lens will not stay against your eye, it could be due to dryness. Use rewetting drops to moisten the eye, making lens placement easier.
  • Blurry vision – For persistent blurriness, ensure the lens is clean and correctly positioned on the cornea. If the problem persists, consult an eye care professional to rule out other issues.

Remember, practice leads to perfection and these steps will feel like second nature after a while.

Tips for First-Time Contact Lens Users

Wearing contact lenses for the first time can seem a little daunting. Here are some simple tips to make the process easier and more comfortable:

  • Wash Your Hands – Always wash and dry your hands before handling your lenses to maintain cleanliness.
  • Use a Mirror – Stand in front of a mirror with good lighting. This helps you see what you're doing.
  • Practice – Regular practice will make the process easier over time.
  • Follow Care Instructions – Adhere to your eye doctor's care instructions to ensure your lenses remain effective and long-lasting.

Whether you’re wearing contacts for vision correction or changing your look with color contacts, first-time users can easily adjust by following these steps.

Maintaining Eye Comfort with Contacts

Eye doctors play a vital role in ensuring user comfort with contact lens. Regular eye exams are essential for identifying issues early. Optometrists ensure lenses fit properly and meet your vision requirements. They also provide guidance on proper lens care.

Always wash your hands before touching your lenses. Use appropriate cleaning solutions and adhere to your eye doctor’s contact replacement schedules. Having regular eye doctor visits and following their advice can help maintain sharp vision and minimize risks.

Enjoy the Benefits of Contact Lenses

Contact lenses offer a convenient alternative for vision correction. Below are a few benefits wearing contacts can have over wearing glasses.

  • Easy to wear – Unlike glasses, contacts provide clear vision without facial fit concerns or regular adjustment, making them a hassle-free option.
  • Athlete-friendly – Contacts don't fog up and let you stay active without concern, making them ideal for sports and physical activities.
  • Unobstructed vision – Contact lenses provide a wider visual field than glasses, as eyewear frames can limit your view.

Adapting to contact lenses may require some time to adjust. But with practice, inserting and removing them becomes simple, and the benefits will outweigh the initial effort.
